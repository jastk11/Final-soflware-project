That Luang

Pha That Luang is located in That Luang village, Xaysettha district, Vientiane Capital, Lao PDR. Originally built in 1566 by King Setthathirat, this 147-foot tall Buddhist monument was built in three tiers to represent the path to enlightenment. With each tier the student learns more Buddhist teachings, narrows their focus, and comes closer to enlightenment. It is a centre of religion and culture and teaches people about Laosâ€™ ancient culture and history.

The stupa is one of the most famous cultural landmarks in Laos, and one among the top tourist attractions and features some of the oldest archeology in Vientiane and the country. The That Luang Festival, held annually on the 15th full moon of the 11th lunar month (November), is Laos' most revered celebration. It honors Pha That Luang and King Xaysetthathirath, whose statue stands in front of the stupa. People from across the country gather to participate in traditional activities and religious ceremonies, including a wax castle procession, almsgiving, traditional Lao hockey, and candlelit processions, all as acts of devotion and homage to the sacred stupa.

Pha That Luang, a sacred stupa in Vientiane, Laos, has origins tracing back to an ancient Cham obelisk built between the 9th and 14th centuries. Archaeological findings, including a statue of King Jayavarman VII (1181â€“1219 AD), support this history.

In 1566, King Saysetthathirath commissioned the construction of the present stupa after making Vientiane the new capital, moving it from Luang Prabang. Inspired by the Grand Stupa in Chiang Mai, he aimed to establish a prominent Buddhist site and demonstrate his devotion. The king also sought to unite his kingdom through an annual festival, testing the loyalty of his administrators and honoring Buddhism and King Fa Ngum, who played a key role in spreading Buddhism in Laos. This tradition continues today as the That Luang Festival.
